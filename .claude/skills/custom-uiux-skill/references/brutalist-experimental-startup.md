# Reference direction: Brutalist / Experimental Startup

Loud, opinionated, on-purpose. Reads as a small team with strong taste,
not a corporation. The UI breaks design-convention rules deliberately to
get attention and signal personality.

## When to use it

- Marketing surfaces for very-online founder-led startups.
- Niche tools that thrive on insider audience signal.
- Experimental media, indie tools, manifesto-style product pages.
- Limited-time launch campaigns where standing out is the whole point.

## When not to use it

- Anything used daily for serious work. Brutalism does not scale to
  workflow surfaces.
- Mainstream consumer products.
- Regulated industries.
- The interior product surfaces of any of the above: usually you use
  brutalism for the marketing site and a different reference for the
  app.

## What it should feel like

Made by humans with taste. Slightly uncomfortable. Unmistakable next to
the templated competitors. The user remembers it.

## Layout traits

- Asymmetry on purpose. Strict grids, then deliberate breaks.
- Oversized type. Display headlines can hit 120–200px on desktop.
- Mixed type sizes inside the same sentence; words as visual objects.
- Overlapping elements, hand-set captions, marginalia, off-grid sticky
  notes.
- Sometimes raw HTML-default cues (default underlines, visible focus
  rings styled deliberately, monospace metadata).

## Color tendencies

- Very high contrast. Pure black on pure white, or one shocking color
  on near-black.
- One loud signature color used everywhere.
- Sometimes two-color print-zine palettes (red + black, blue + cream).
- Avoid gradients. Solid blocks of color.

## Typography tendencies

- A weird display face used with confidence (mono-display, condensed
  sans, distorted serif, variable type set extreme).
- A workhorse mono or sans for the body.
- Hard-set tracking and leading. Mixed weights mid-sentence allowed.
- Sentence-as-design. The words look like they were *placed*, not
  flowed.

## Component patterns

- Buttons can be raw rectangles with a thick black border and a hard
  shadow.
- Tables exposed and unstyled, or styled to look intentionally
  newspaper-like.
- Stickers, stamps, scribbles, scanned objects as accent imagery.
- Marquee strips for announcements (motion-reduced when appropriate).
- "Sentence-link" patterns where copy *is* the navigation.

## Navigation patterns

- Minimal. Sometimes a single sentence at the top is the nav.
- "About / Work / Contact" with no chrome.
- Footer is loud, sometimes louder than the nav.

## Motion / interaction style

- Big, dramatic, hand-crafted. A page transition can be a fullscreen
  word fly-out.
- Custom cursors on desktop (with respect for input devices).
- Marquee scroll for announcements.
- Always: respect reduced motion. Brutalism does *not* exempt you.

## Good inspiration categories

- Indie design studios' own sites.
- Print zine and risograph publication aesthetics.
- Conference and album launch landing pages.
- Manifesto-style product pages from very-online startups.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- Brutalism applied to a checkout flow. Don't.
- Inaccessible contrast just to look edgy (always pass WCAG AA).
- Performance disasters from huge custom fonts and uncompressed images.
- Auto-playing audio.
- Custom cursors that hurt usability on touch / accessibility tools.
- Removing focus indicators because they "look generic."
- Brutalism as decoration over a generic SaaS skeleton: that reads as
  costume, not character.

## Example suitable products

- A founder's manifesto / launch page.
- An indie tool with strong personality (a writing tool, a calendar).
- A creative agency's site.
- A limited-edition product drop.
- A media brand for an under-served niche.

## Example unsuitable products

- A medical product.
- A finance dashboard.
- A B2B onboarding flow.
- A children's product.
- A long-running productivity tool's main app surface.
