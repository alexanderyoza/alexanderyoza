# Reference direction: Wellness Minimal

Health, fitness, sleep, supplement, and mental-health products that
must feel trustworthy *and* welcoming. Cleaner than playful-consumer,
warmer than calm-Japanese. The body-care category aesthetic.

## When to use it

- Fitness, sleep, and recovery tracking apps.
- Mental-health products targeting adults.
- Supplement, vitamin, and DTC wellness brands.
- Patient-facing healthtech (with extra clinical-credibility cues).

## When not to use it

- Clinical or regulated medical devices: those need a different
  reference with stronger compliance signaling.
- Pure productivity tools.
- High-density data tools.
- Children's products.

## What it should feel like

Calm but capable. The UI looks like it could help you. It does not feel
like a hospital, and it does not feel like a juice ad.

## Layout traits

- Generous spacing; cards have meaningful hierarchy.
- A "today" hub as the home screen: one or two primary numbers, a
  short list of actions.
- Detail screens reveal more depth without dumping it all at once.
- Mobile-first; bottom tab bar with 3–4 destinations.

## Color tendencies

- Cream / off-white surfaces. Sometimes a sage / olive / dusty green
  neutral as a second surface.
- One signature accent: often a muted green, ochre, blush, or sand.
- Functional reds and ambers used sparingly for warnings.
- Soft gradients allowed *only* on hero illustrations / loading states,
  never on flat surfaces.

## Typography tendencies

- A friendly humanist sans (Inter, DM Sans, Söhne).
- Sometimes paired with a quiet serif for headers.
- Body 16–17px, leading 1.6.
- Numbers feel approachable: not tabular finance-mono, not arcade.

## Component patterns

- Streak / consistency indicators that are dignified, not gamified.
- "Today's plan" card on the home screen.
- Modules that pair a number with a one-sentence interpretation
  ("You slept 7h12m, slightly above your week average").
- Onboarding that earns trust before asking for permissions.
- Permission prompts that explain *why* in one sentence before the OS
  modal appears.

## Navigation patterns

- Bottom tab bar (mobile) with named icons.
- Top app bar minimal: usually just the screen name.
- Logging actions are big and reachable; the user opens the app to do
  one thing, the UI should respect that.

## Motion / interaction style

- 200–300ms ease-out.
- Soft fades, gentle slides.
- Breath / pulse loops allowed on focal elements.
- Confetti / celebration limited to milestone moments (week streaks),
  not daily.
- Reduced motion turns off the pulse loops.

## Good inspiration categories

- Modern DTC supplement brands.
- Sleep and recovery apps (category-level).
- Therapy-adjacent apps that have earned credibility.
- Hydration and habit apps.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- Stock photos of laughing fit people on a yoga mat.
- Score-based gamification (Levels! XP!). Wellness is not a video game.
- Aggressive nudges and guilt-trip notifications.
- Bright neon accents.
- "Streak lost!" UI that punishes a missed day.
- Mass-marketing pop-ups in the middle of a check-in flow.
- AI sparkle icons on every feature.

## Example suitable products

- A sleep tracking app.
- A daily mood / mental-health check-in.
- A supplement or vitamin DTC site.
- A consumer-friendly fitness coaching app.
- A nutrition tracker for adults.

## Example unsuitable products

- A clinical EMR.
- A trading dashboard.
- A children's game.
- A devtool.
- A long-form reading site.
