# Reference direction: Luxury Commerce

High-end retail. The product photographs sell. The UI exists to get out
of the way of the merchandise and to confer status. Pages feel like a
flagship store, not a discount marketplace.

## When to use it

- High-end fashion, jewelry, watches, art, hotels, travel.
- Direct-to-consumer brands positioned premium-and-up.
- Heritage and craft brands.
- Curated marketplaces with editorial taste.

## When not to use it

- Volume retail. The aesthetic kills conversion at mass-market price
  points.
- Utility products. Luxury reads as inappropriate.
- B2B. The gravity reads as marketing.

## What it should feel like

Quiet confidence. The brand does not shout the price. The whitespace
*is* the price. The user feels welcomed into a world.

## Layout traits

- Full-bleed imagery; hero is a single editorial photograph.
- Type set against generous whitespace; aligned to a confident grid.
- Product detail pages use a slow vertical reveal: image, then story,
  then specs, then purchase.
- Two-column PDP on desktop (gallery + sticky details) is acceptable,
  but the gallery dominates.
- Mobile is single-column, with imagery filling 100% width.

## Color tendencies

- Off-white / cream / pearl background. Pure white is rare.
- Ink, deep navy, espresso, or olive as the type color.
- One restrained brand color used very sparingly.
- Gold/foil-like accents only if the brand earns them, and never as
  CSS gradients.

## Typography tendencies

- A high-contrast serif (Didone family, Bodoni, Didot, or a refined
  modern serif like Tiempos Headline).
- A small-caps, generous-tracked sans for nav and metadata.
- Numerals are tabular and small; prices are not screamed.
- Body 16–18px, leading generous (1.6–1.8).

## Component patterns

- Editorial-style category landing pages ("The Spring Edit").
- Sticky add-to-bag bar on PDP that feels invisible until needed.
- Wishlist as a quiet bookmark icon, not a heart with confetti.
- Concierge / contact module on PDP for high-ticket items.
- Lookbook-style multi-photo modules.
- Pricing displayed without a strikethrough discount-shouter.

## Navigation patterns

- Slim top nav; logotype centered or left, single hairline rule beneath.
- Mega-menu rendered as a tasteful column of words, not a grid of
  product images.
- Footer is long and tasteful: care, craft, story, customer service.
- Cart is a slide-over, never a clunky page navigation.

## Motion / interaction style

- Slow, considered fades: 250–400ms.
- Lazy crossfades between gallery images.
- No bouncing. No spring.
- Reveal-on-scroll for editorial sections, with reduced-motion fallback.
- Hover treatments are restrained: image swap, hairline appears.

## Good inspiration categories

- Heritage fashion houses' web flagships (read patterns, not assets).
- Independent jewelry studios.
- Boutique hotel brand sites.
- High-end watch and leather goods sites.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- Bright "SALE!" stickers. Discounts, if any, are discreet.
- Animated countdown timers on PDP.
- "X people are looking at this" social-proof bars.
- Pop-up "10% off your first order!" modals on load.
- Skinny CTA buttons with neon hover states.
- Sparkle / gradient backgrounds anywhere.
- Stock fashion photography.
- Trustpilot-style badges shoved into the hero.

## Example suitable products

- A high-end watch boutique.
- A heritage menswear or womenswear brand.
- A jewelry studio.
- A boutique hotel brand.
- An art / print gallery.

## Example unsuitable products

- A fast-fashion ecommerce store.
- A discount marketplace.
- A B2B procurement portal.
- An internal admin tool.
- A children's product.
