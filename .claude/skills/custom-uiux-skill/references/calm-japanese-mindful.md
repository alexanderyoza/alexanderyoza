# Reference direction: Calm Japanese / Mindful

Quiet, deliberate, generous-with-air UI. Inspired by Japanese design
sensibility (ma, the use of negative space) and contemplative product
categories. The user should feel slower, not faster, after using it.

## When to use it

- Meditation, journaling, sleep, breathing, mood, reflective writing.
- Slow learning products (a language app that respects the user's time).
- Wellness, mindfulness, intentional-living, slow-tech apps.
- Reading apps where ease and quiet matter more than speed.

## When not to use it

- High-velocity workflows, dashboards, or social feeds.
- Any product whose user value is "doing more, faster."
- Markets where bright color and density signal trust (some commerce).
- Mobile games and entertainment.

## What it should feel like

Calm. Spacious. The page is not asking for anything. Each screen does
one thing, and does it without urgency.

## Layout traits

- Generous whitespace; small content, big margins.
- One primary action per screen at most.
- Single column even on wide screens; max content width often < 640px.
- Vertical rhythm honored aggressively; line-height tall.
- Subtle horizontal rules and section breaks; no heavy dividers.

## Color tendencies

- Off-white / paper background. Pure white is too bright.
- Ink black or deep indigo for type. Never pure #000.
- One restrained accent, usually a desaturated red, indigo, or a warm
  earth tone (kinari, sumi, beni).
- Dark mode is a deep slate/charcoal, never pitch black.
- No gradients. No neon.

## Typography tendencies

- A humanist serif or a refined Japanese-inspired sans (Source Han,
  Noto, IBM Plex Sans JP if working multilingual).
- Body size moderate, generous leading (1.7–1.9).
- Tabular figures for dates and counts.
- Numerals are quiet, not gigantic.
- Heading scale is small: display sizes rarely exceed 40px.

## Component patterns

- "Single card per screen" pattern for breath and journal screens.
- Quiet text-only buttons; primary action often a small, centered pill.
- Time-of-day responsive surfaces (lighter morning, dimmer evening).
- Streaks and counts shown small and dignified, not gamified.
- Iconography is minimal, often a single line weight.

## Navigation patterns

- Few destinations. 2–4 tabs maximum.
- A "today" surface as the home: single piece of content, no feed.
- Settings tucked behind a small text link, not a wheel icon shouting.

## Motion / interaction style

- Slow. 300–500ms ease-out fades. Nothing bounces.
- Breath-like loops on focal elements (e.g., the breathing circle).
- Page transitions are dissolves, not slides.
- Reduced motion mode disables even the breath loops: the user opted
  into stillness.

## Good inspiration categories

- Japanese stationery and product packaging.
- Meditation and mindfulness apps (category-level).
- Tea-ceremony adjacent product brands.
- Small Japanese design studios' web presence.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- Imposing a fake Asian aesthetic via clip-art cherry blossoms or
  kanji-as-decoration. That's not Japanese-inspired, that's offensive.
- Gamification (streaks, levels, badges with confetti).
- Push notifications that interrupt.
- Heavy modals or full-screen takeovers.
- Animated counters and "scoring" UI.
- Bright accent colors used decoratively.

## Example suitable products

- A meditation / breathwork app.
- A daily journaling product.
- A slow-paced language learning app (especially Japanese, Korean,
  Mandarin learning).
- A long-form reading and annotation tool.
- A "one-task-per-day" personal planner.

## Example unsuitable products

- A live sports betting app.
- An advertising analytics dashboard.
- A social photo-sharing feed.
- A real-time chat tool for teams.
- A POS or warehouse-scanning app.
