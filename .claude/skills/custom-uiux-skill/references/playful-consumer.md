# Reference direction: Playful Consumer

Bright, expressive, emotionally warm products. The UI smiles back at the
user. Best for things people use because they *want to*, not because
they're paid to.

## When to use it

- Habit, hobby, lifestyle, and small-joy apps.
- Onboarding flows for consumer products targeting non-experts.
- Children's and family products (with extra a11y rigor).
- Anything that depends on emotional pull more than on power features.

## When not to use it

- Serious money, health, or legal contexts where users want gravity.
- Pro tools: playful reads as untrustworthy at scale.
- High-density data work.
- Anyone over 35 in a B2B setting.

## What it should feel like

Friendly, soft, alive. The first impression is "this is for me and it
will be fun." Not childish: confident in its warmth.

## Layout traits

- Generous padding; chunky cards with strong silhouettes.
- Off-grid arrangements; one diagonal or asymmetric move per screen.
- Hero is character-led: a mascot, an oversized object, a hand-drawn
  graphic, or a custom illustration. Not a product screenshot.
- Sticky bottom CTA bar on mobile.

## Color tendencies

- Warm and saturated, but not neon. Cream/butter backgrounds beat pure
  white.
- 1 hero color + 1 surprise accent + 1 calm neutral.
- Allowed: peach, marigold, sage, terracotta, dusty pink, periwinkle.
- Avoid: corporate blue + grey, glassy purples, dot-grid backgrounds.

## Typography tendencies

- Rounded sans or geometric humanist as the body (Inter Rounded, DM
  Sans, Söhne, Recoleta for display).
- Display face often has personality: a soft serif or a quirky
  geometric.
- Larger sizes than productivity defaults: 18px body minimum.
- Sentence case in buttons. Microcopy reads like a friendly note.

## Component patterns

- Pill buttons with light shadows or soft borders, never sharp edges.
- Sticker-like badges (slight rotation, hand-drawn feel).
- Illustration-led empty states with personality (no "no data" graphs).
- Cards with character: a colored top edge, a mascot peek, an emoji-as-
  icon used sparingly.
- Confetti / micro-celebration moments on key wins (with reduced-motion
  fallback).

## Navigation patterns

- Bottom tab bar (mobile) with clear, named tabs (icons + labels).
- Top nav (web) with a generous logo and a single warm CTA.
- "Home" feels like a feed of warmth, not a dashboard.

## Motion / interaction style

- Spring physics, light bounces on press.
- Drag-to-dismiss, satisfying haptics, success animations.
- Page transitions can be expressive but must respect reduced motion.
- Loading states feel alive (drawn animations, not spinners).

## Good inspiration categories

- Consumer habit apps (categories: habit tracking, journaling, mood,
  music discovery, social fitness).
- Indie iOS apps and App Store editorial.
- Children's product packaging.
- Modern stationery brands.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- "Cute" that becomes patronizing: talk *to* users, not down to them.
- Default emojis pasted in as icons.
- Pastels with poor contrast on white.
- Confetti on every action.
- Mascots that block content.
- Trying to be playful in error and security copy ("Oopsie!" on a
  payment failure is wrong).

## Example suitable products

- A habit-tracking app.
- A consumer journaling app.
- A language learning app for casual learners.
- A small-group social/messaging app.
- A children's reading product.

## Example unsuitable products

- A tax filing tool.
- A clinical health record.
- A trading desk.
- An enterprise admin console.
- A legal-document signing flow.
